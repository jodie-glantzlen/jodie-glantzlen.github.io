My long term career pathway

I can think of two main options for me. Either work for a web-development or SAAS company, or work freelance on personal projects my partner and I have. It’s too early to say, because even though I’m enjoying this course so far and I like front-end development, I have no idea what being a web developer really involves (I have never done back-end, or database, or even languages other than JS, CSS and HTML). Maybe I’ll realise being in the heart of tech is not for me and I’ll turn to other options while staying in the industry.

Long story short, I don’t know! One step at a time is my motto.

My learning style: strengths and limits

My strong empathetic nature helps me understand people’s needs and feelings. I’m good at always choosing my words carefully or behaving in a mindful way. This makes me a good team player. I’ve always thought learning and growing together as a group is more effective and powerful, which is the main reason why I enrolled in this course. I’ve tried twice to learn on my own and never succeeded. However, as an INFJ, I’m very sensitive and prone to stress and anxiety. This can be both a strength and weakness. I’ve used stress before as a really effective motivator. But in negative environments where I don’t feel supported or listened to, it sucks all my energy and motivation away and tends to freeze me. It all comes down again to empathy and finding the right fit!

Human skills: a new hope

I hope to work on my self-confidence and truly embrace the growth mindset. I’ve always convinced myself that my brain was not logical, that I was not “nerdy” enough. I want to deconstruct my set beliefs, and stop thinking “you can’t do it”. I want to not only tell myself “you CAN do it, It’ll take time, work and effort, it won’t be easy, but it is possible”, but actually believe it and act on it. I know I need to be more forgiving towards myself, because I deserve it!

Workload management

Working productively and safely with others

Something that works well for me is a strict routine, and knowing when to stop. I will set boundaries between study/work time and home time. I don’t mind staying late on the EDA campus (if we’re allowed to), but as soon as I come home, it’s home time.

What to do when things start to build up and get to me

I will disconnect for a moment and do something completely non-tech related, so that my brain can focus on something lighter and let the new information sink in in the background. When I learned the basics of javascript for the first time, I spent hours stuck on the concept of nested arrays and loops. I dit not allow myself to rest until I would understand it (which did NOT work). That day, I went to bed angry and frustrated. After a good night’s sleep though, it magically clicked in the morning. So I know taking some distance is healthy and necessary when you learn heavy subjects for the first time.

Asking for help

A good programmer needs to know how to Google, so I will try to figure things out myself first. If it takes too long and I’m still stuck, I will ask my peers or tutors/facilitators for help. That’s what they’re here for, especially as we’re students in a learning environment (I know you never really stop learning when you’re a professional, but when everything is new, asking for help is essential).

What I expect from the facilitation team

It's a long and intense journey ahead of us, so I think everyone will need to feel supported mentally (they're already doing a great job at that!). Additionally, when I feel stuck, I would love to be guided towards a solution to my problem, to help me find it myself, rather than being given the answer.

Other commitments

I’m lucky enough to be able to fully commit to this course, I don’t have anything planned for the next three months.
